About FLL
What is FIRST LEGO League?

FIRST LEGO League is a global robotics competition program designed to inspire young people to think like scientists and engineers. Every season, teams compete across four major areas: the Robot Game, where an autonomous LEGO robot completes missions on a themed mat; Robot Design, which evaluates the engineering and construction of the robot itself; the Innovation Project, where teams research and present a solution to a real-world problem; and Core Values, which celebrates teamwork, inclusion, and gracious professionalism both on and off the competition floor.

Season 2: Unearthed
Our robot, innovation project, and core values for the 2025–2026 season

LEGO SPIKE Prime  ·  PyBricks  ·  Projekt Alpha Drive Base

Our Season 2 robot is built on the Projekt Alpha drive base by NxtGen Robotics. After Season 1's bulky robot suffered wheel slippage and navigation drift from battery-dependent SPIKE coding, we completely redesigned from the ground up.

  • Drive base: Compact enclosed body — rubber driving wheels for grip, plastic non-drive wheels for straight tracking, low center of gravity to prevent getting stuck
  • Quick-swap attachments: Standardized mount allows fast mission transitions with no lost seconds
  • PyBricks: Gyro-based navigation eliminates battery-level drift; true multi-tasking support; works in both Python and block coding
  • Motor reset: Each run resets the arm to a physical stop and squares the base against the rear wall — identical starting position every time
  • Accurate turns: Custom turn function runs both wheels in the same direction to compensate gear backlash, critical for Mission 4's precision recovery angle

Best score: 530 pts  ·  7 runs  ·  ~86% success rate  ·  145s total run time

We planned 7 runs, each owned by one team member. Routes were carefully designed to minimize travel distance and number of turns. After the qualifier we reordered runs, prioritized reliable ones first, and added Missions 11 & 15 to the regional plan. Total run time: 107.9s at ~86% success rate.

Our Problem Statement
How can we safely explore inaccessible archaeology sites without destroying them?
The Inspiration

Our team was inspired by a visit to Emperor Qin Shi Huang's (秦始皇) Terracotta Army and his tomb. We learned that the tomb itself has never been opened — it contains dangerously high concentrations of mercury. This sparked our question: how many more historic sites around the world are simply too dangerous or inaccessible to explore?

The Challenge: Inaccessible & Unsafe Sites
⚠️ Structural hazards — collapsing walls, unstable platforms (e.g. Jiankou section of the Great Wall)
☠️ Atmospheric dangers — toxic gases, mercury (e.g. Qin Shi Huang's tomb)
🧪 Biological & chemical hazards — mold, pathogens, chemical contamination
💣 Physical & logistical barriers — landmines (Sudanese temples), war zones (20,000+ Middle East sites)

And even when excavation is possible — invasive digging destroys the site at the same time. We need a better way.

Austin and Archer led our initial research into archaeology pain points. We consulted webinars, books, and websites to understand the full scope of the problem and existing technologies.

Key Finding

Over 20,000 archaeology sites in the Middle East and North Africa are inaccessible due to war. Over 500 sites in Ukraine are damaged. Cave sites like parts of the Grand Canyon are unreachable due to flash floods and steep cliffs. The problem is global and urgent.

We reached out to four professional archaeologists and geology experts for feedback on our idea. Their insights directly shaped our final design.

John Rick
Associate Professor of Anthropology, Stanford University

Provided foundational perspective on the challenges archaeologists face in the field and the limitations of current tools.

Ervan Garrison
Professor Emeritus of Archaeological Geology & Geophysics, University of Georgia

Offered expertise on geophysical surveying and non-invasive archaeological methods.

Katie Chin-Quee
Museum & Heritage Sector, Collections Care & Public Engagement, Durham University, UK

Shared the perspective of museum professionals and conservators on artifact handling and preservation.

Shelby Medina
Anthropology, UC Berkeley

Called our idea "innovative" and "cutting edge." Key concerns raised: waterproofing for field conditions, and weak wireless signal underground.

What We Learned
  • Robot use in archaeology is considered cutting edge by professionals
  • The rover needs to be waterproof for real field conditions
  • Wireless signal is too weak underground — our AI arm iteration directly addresses this by operating autonomously without a signal
Arm Iteration 1

Human-Shaped Robotic Arm

A gesture-manipulation arm that mirrors the operator's hand movements in real time — inspired by the Da Vinci Robotic Surgical System used in minimally invasive surgery.

✓ Pros
  • Follows hand gestures intuitively
  • Precise movement and gentle touch
  • Force and haptic feedback possible
  • Good for regularly shaped objects
✗ Cons
  • Requires very precise positioning
  • Can only grab small objects
  • Not flexible for irregular shapes

Arm Iteration 2

Octopus-Shaped Robotic Arm

Inspired by the SpiRobs research paper, this arm mimics an octopus tentacle — 3D-printed in flexible TPU material, controlled by two Kevlar cords that bend it left and right.

Key engineering decisions: PLA was too rigid and broke easily → switched to TPU (flexible and durable). Regular sewing thread wasn't strong enough → switched to Kevlar cord (high strength, low stretch). A rigid extruding base extends the arm's reach and prevents objects from hitting the rover body.

✓ Pros
  • Bends around irregular shapes
  • Good for round or oddly shaped objects
  • Flexible — won't damage fragile artifacts
✗ Cons
  • Must be positioned very accurately
  • Requires wireless signal to operate
Arm Iteration 3

AI-Controlled Robot Arm

After learning from robot expert Hao Li, we adopted the open-source LeRobot SO-101 arm. Andy proposed and Andy & Austin set up the arms and trained the AI models.

  • Collected 400 episodes of teleoperation training data
  • Data hosted on HuggingFace (huggingface.co/fanaustinca) and shared with the AI/ML community
  • Trained two models: ACT (Action Chunking Transformers) and Diffusion Policy
  • Results: ACT 150 episodes performed best; Diffusion 400 episodes underperformed
  • Controlled using Claude + Gemini LLMs via camera feed — made a control panel using Claude Code
✓ Pros
  • Fully autonomous — no signal needed
  • Solves the underground signal problem
✗ Cons
  • Slow — 2 min to pick up one brick
  • Camera jitter and blind spots
  • Can only grab small objects
  • Not easily moved once set up

After Regionals — Next Iteration

Computer Vision + Inverse Kinematics

Instead of training on teleoperation data, we developed a fully analytical pipeline — no machine learning required for the motion itself:

Step 1 — 3D Localization

An image processing model analyzes the camera feed to detect artifacts. Stereo triangulation from two camera viewpoints calculates the artifact's exact position in 3D space.

Step 2 — Inverse Kinematics

Given the target 3D coordinates, inverse kinematics solves the joint angles needed to position the end-effector precisely — eliminating the need for thousands of training episodes.

Our Solution
Relic Rover — History Recover
A remote-controlled rover that safely explores inaccessible archaeology sites

The Relic Rover combines a wheeled rover with three sensor and manipulation systems to explore sites safely without damaging them:

  • Unitree 4D LiDAR sensor — creates a 3D map of the site without physical contact
  • Camera — provides visual inspection for remote operators
  • 3D-printed TPU Octopus Arm — wraps gently around fragile artifacts of any shape using Kevlar cords
  • AI-controlled robot arm (LeRobot SO-101) — operates autonomously underground where wireless signal is too weak
Non-invasive AI-powered LiDAR mapping Open-source data

Our Impact

The Relic Rover enables safe, cost-efficient, non-invasive exploration of dangerous or inaccessible archaeology sites — protecting both researchers and irreplaceable history. We shared our project at the Bay Area Screammage, with FLL teams in Arizona, and at the FLL Spring League. Our 400-episode training dataset is publicly available on HuggingFace for the global AI/ML community.
